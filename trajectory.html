Meridian trajectory preview

Showing how risk changes both whether an event happens and when it tends to happen.

This page is a reviewer artifact for the trajectory concept. The intent is to test whether clinicians can understand one idea quickly: event timing is incomplete unless it is paired with the denominator. For every endpoint, Meridian eventually needs to answer both questions: what share of comparable people experience the event by the horizon, and when does it usually occur among those who do?

Clinical status

Communication prototype. The values are illustrative placeholders. This is not a patient-specific forecast and not yet a validated Meridian model output.

Intent

The purpose of this view is not to convince anyone that Meridian has finalized these numbers. The purpose is to make the forecast object honest before the model is finalized.

A conventional event window says, for example, that a first heart attack or stroke tends to occur around a certain age. That is only a conditional timing statement. It applies to people who have the event. The missing denominator is the share of comparable people who have the event at all by the modeled horizon.

This page tests a single visual grammar for combining those two pieces. The triangle base shows the central 80% timing interval, from the 10th to 90th percentile of event timing among people who experience the endpoint by the horizon. The triangle height shows cumulative incidence by the horizon. Moving from a higher-risk profile to a lower-risk profile can therefore do two things at once: push the event later and reduce the share of people who ever enter that event distribution.

Question 1What percent of comparable people have this event by the horizon?
Question 2Among people who have it, when does it tend to occur?
Question 3Does a lower-risk profile change incidence, timing, or both?

Method, in plain English

This version compares two cohort profiles, not a treatment effect. The higher-risk row represents a worse modeled risk state for a 45 to 50 year-old ATM positive, dyslipidemic male reference cohort. The selected-risk row moves along a continuum toward a lower-risk profile. The page does not claim that Meridian interventions have caused that movement.

For each endpoint, the prototype stores four placeholder quantities: a median event age, a central 80% timing interval around that median, cumulative incidence by age 95 for a higher-risk profile, and cumulative incidence by age 95 for a lower-risk profile. The slider interpolates between the two risk profiles. It shifts the timing window later and lowers the cumulative incidence.

These are marginal endpoint estimates. They are not mutually exclusive, not a competing-risk adjusted survival model, and not a single multi-state sequence. Those are production modeling requirements, not finished work in this artifact.

Autonomy curve, first pass

This section connects the slider to the idea of bending the curve. The autonomy curve is not a new disease endpoint. It is a bounded 0 to 100 functional independence trajectory anchored to established external constructs: EQ-5D or WHODAS for health utility and disability, PROMIS Physical Function for function, Katz ADL and Lawton IADL for hard independence thresholds, and frailty index logic for reserve and slope.

In this prototype, each endpoint can affect the curve in two ways. First, it contributes an expected autonomy impact weighted by its cumulative incidence. Second, when risk improves, the event shifts later and its expected impact begins later. The slider therefore bends the curve by preserving reserve, reducing expected event burden, and delaying event-related slope changes. The curve is intentionally truncated at the age 95 horizon and should not be visually extrapolated beyond that boundary.

Trajectory chart

Read each triangle as one endpoint forecast. Width is timing: the 10th to 90th percentile interval among people who experience the endpoint by age 95. Height is incidence. Apex is median event age among those who experience the endpoint. The numbers are placeholders so reviewers can judge the communication structure before the final model exists.

Important: evaluate the representation, not the placeholder values. The chart is designed to show the shape of the forecast object that Meridian needs to model, not to assert these exact risks.
Encoding note: triangle base spans the central 80% conditional timing interval, p10 to p90, among people who experience the endpoint by age 95. Triangle area is not interpreted as probability because its units would mix years and percent. In production, the chart should add uncertainty intervals once the event models are calibrated.

Endpoint definitions

These definitions are deliberately visible because MD reviewers should be able to challenge the endpoint object itself. MACE and cancer are cleaner event objects. Mobility, independent living, and MCI or dementia need stricter operational definitions before production use.

What this version fixes

  • It explains intent first.The first section tells the reader why the page exists before showing the chart.
  • It separates communication concept from model truth.The page says the values are placeholders and invites review of the representation.
  • It defines the horizon.Incidence means cumulative incidence by age 95 in this prototype.
  • It separates risk-state comparison from plan effect.Lower-risk profile is not presented as a proven causal result of Meridian interventions.
  • It discloses marginal endpoints.The page says the events are not mutually exclusive and not yet competing-risk adjusted.

What would still get challenged

  • The placeholder numbers.They need to be replaced by calibrated model outputs with confidence intervals.
  • Competing risks.Death and prior events need explicit treatment before this becomes clinical truth.
  • Intervention attribution.If Meridian claims movement from high to low risk, the causal pathway must be shown.
  • Functional endpoints.Mobility loss and independent living need stricter measurement rules.
  • Endpoint dependencies.Stroke, dementia, mobility loss, cancer, and independence are clinically entangled.

Calculation notes for reviewers who want the mechanics

For each endpoint e, the prototype stores: medianAgeHigh, p10AgeHigh, p90AgeHigh, incidenceHighBy95, maxMedianShift, incidenceLowBy95.
The displayed base is the central 80% conditional timing interval: p10 to p90 among cohort members who experience the endpoint by age 95.
Slider scale s ranges from 0 to 1. At s = 0, selected profile equals the higher-risk profile. At s = 1, selected profile equals the lower-risk profile.
selectedMedianAge = medianAgeHigh + maxMedianShift x s.
selectedIncidenceBy95 = incidenceHighBy95 minus ((incidenceHighBy95 minus incidenceLowBy95) x s).
selectedP10Age = p10AgeHigh + maxMedianShift x s. selectedP90Age = p90AgeHigh + maxMedianShift x s, with optional widening only if the calibrated model shows wider late-age uncertainty.
Autonomy prototype: autonomy(age) = baseline reserve decline minus late-life terminal compression minus the sum of event impact weights multiplied by each endpoint's cumulative incidence and a smoothed timing transition around that endpoint's median age.
Slider effect on autonomy: lower-risk movement reduces early decline, lowers event incidence weights, and shifts event transitions later. It does not remove terminal aging and the curve is not extrapolated beyond the age 95 horizon.
